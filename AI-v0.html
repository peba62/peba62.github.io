Discipline Area: Artificial Intelligence

The goal of Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the creation of intelligent software and other systems by computational means, through algorithms and programs that exhibit intelligent behaviour, for perception, reasoning/inference, and action.

NICTA hosts a substantial body of AI research through a large number of researchers and specifically targeted projects (see here for a list of all current NICTA projects). The purpose of this page is to provide a complementary, high-level overview from the viewpoint of the subfields of AI represented at NICTA. These subfields are "Constraint Satisfaction, Satisfiability, Search", "Knowledge Representation, Reasoning, Logic", "Machine Learning", and "Planning and Scheduling".

Constraint Satisfaction, Satisfiability, Search

Constraint satisfaction, in its most general description, is the process of finding a solution to a set of constraints that impose conditions that the variables must satisfy. By satisfiability we mean research questions around the satisfibility of propositional logic, such as the design of efficient algorithms (SAT solving). By search we mean research into the design and analysis of general search algorithms.

At NICTA we carry out pure and interrelated research in these areas, which includes theoretical foundations, implementations, and application to real-world problems.

Knowledge Representation, Reasoning, Logic

The area of knowledge representation, reasoning, logic is concerned with formally modeling a domain of interest using mathematical logic and corresponding reasoning methods. Research questions concern the design, analysis, implementation and application of such reasoning methods, which can be interactive or automated and are parametrized by a specific logic and reasoning services.

While "Constraint Satisfaction, Satisfiability, Search" is predominantly concerned with finite variable domains and finite search, "knowledge representation, reasoning, logic" focuses on expressive logics that typically do not admit a finite search space.

At NICTA, research spans reasoning methods on a variety of logics, viz., first-order, higher-order, classical and nonmonotonic logics.
